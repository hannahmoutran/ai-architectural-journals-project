ISSUE DESCRIPTION:
--------------------
This May 1917 issue of The Southern Architect offers a comprehensive snapshot of early 20th-century architectural and construction innovations, with a particular focus on the American South's evolving built environment amid World War I. It features extensive advertisements and articles highlighting advancements in building materials such as concrete construction, structural steel, and roofing technologies including metal shingles and roofing slate. Noteworthy are discussions on heating and ventilation systems, including forced air heating and warm air furnaces, reflecting growing attention to building comfort and energy efficiency. The issue also addresses sanitary engineering innovations, including chemical closets and sewerage systems, underscoring public health concerns in suburban and urban development. Architectural firms and prominent architects like G. Lloyd Preacher and Sanguinet & Staats are noted for their contributions to Southern residential, church, and institutional architecture, with emphasis on Gothic Revival and industrial architectural styles. Articles on architectural practice, professional ethics, and civil service examinations reveal the period's professionalization trends. Significant coverage of sculptural arts, featuring Henry Hering and Richard Engelmann, connects architectural ornamentation with broader artistic movements such as realism and classicism. Urban planning and community development discussions, including the call for city planning boards in Atlanta, situate architecture within civic improvement efforts. The issue also documents the impact of World War I on construction industries and advertising, highlighting economic and material challenges. Overall, this issue is a rich resource for understanding technological, stylistic, and professional developments shaping Southern architecture during a pivotal historical moment.
